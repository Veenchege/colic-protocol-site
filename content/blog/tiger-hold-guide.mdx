## What the Tiger Hold actually does

Most colic guides tell you the Tiger Hold position. Very few explain the mechanism. The mechanism is why it works - and understanding it means you will execute it correctly the first time instead of doing it wrong for two weeks.

The Tiger Hold addresses two of the three colic root cause systems simultaneously:

**System 1: Nervous system dysregulation.** The face-down position combined with a rhythmic pulse at 60 beats per minute deactivates the Moro startle reflex. The Moro reflex is the involuntary full-body startle response present in all newborns. In a colic infant, this reflex activates at a lower threshold than normal, keeping the nervous system in a chronic threat state. The specific combination of prone position and rhythmic vestibular input at resting heartbeat frequency suppresses this reflex activation.

**System 2: Trapped gas.** The face-down position places the baby's belly against your forearm, and the firm but gentle pressure assists peristaltic movement of trapped gas toward the large intestine. Gravity works in the correct direction. The rhythmic pulse amplifies this mechanical effect.

## The exact position

**Step 1 - Positioning your arm.** Hold your non-dominant forearm parallel to the floor, palm facing up. The muscle of your forearm (not your wrist bones) is the contact surface. This matters - wrist contact is uncomfortable for the baby and does not create the abdominal pressure needed.

**Step 2 - Placing the baby.** Lay your baby face-down along your forearm. Their belly rests on the muscle of your arm. Their head rests in your palm, turned to one side. Their legs hang either side of your elbow.

**Step 3 - The rhythm.** Begin a forward-and-back pulse at exactly 60 beats per minute. This is one beat per second - count it. The motion is not rocking side to side. It is a gentle forward pulse. The distinction matters because side-to-side rocking does not create the same vestibular input that suppresses the Moro reflex.

**Step 4 - Duration.** Hold for a minimum of five minutes before assessing results. Most parents stop at 60 to 90 seconds. The nervous system regulation effect requires sustained rhythmic input to interrupt the cortisol loop. Results before five minutes are coincidental. Results at five to seven minutes are the protocol working.

## The most common execution errors

**Wrong surface contact.** Wrist bones instead of forearm muscle. The baby's belly needs the padded muscle surface to receive abdominal pressure without discomfort.

**Wrong rhythm.** Rocking instead of pulsing. Side-to-side instead of forward-back. Put a metronome on your phone at 60 BPM and match it exactly.

**Wrong duration assessment.** Stopping at two minutes because it is not working. The hold requires five minutes minimum.

**Wrong sequence.** Applying the Tiger Hold before the acoustic layer. Brown noise at 60 to 65 decibels must be in place before you pick up your baby. An acoustically dysregulated nervous system cannot receive touch input as calming. Touch before sound adds a sensory demand to an already overloaded system.

## Why the sound layer comes first

This is the sequencing rule that most colic content misses.

Before you pick up your baby, before any physical contact, activate brown noise at 60 to 65 decibels - approximately the volume of a shower - positioned at least seven feet from your baby's head.

Give it 30 seconds.

The acoustic environment signals neurological safety before the physical intervention begins. Your baby's nervous system spent nine months calibrated to low-frequency sounds: maternal heartbeat, blood flow, digestion. Brown noise sits within that frequency range. White noise does not - it sits above the womb frequency range and can actively worsen dysregulation in a colicky infant.

Once the acoustic layer is in place, apply the Tiger Hold. The sequence: sound, then touch, then light adjustment. In that order, every time.

## What the Tiger Hold does not fix

The Tiger Hold manages a colic episode. It does not fix the root cause.

If your baby requires the Tiger Hold every evening and it provides relief for 20 to 40 minutes before crying resumes, the primary driver is likely gut microbiome imbalance. The inflammatory environment in the gut is producing gas continuously. The Tiger Hold moves trapped gas mechanically. It cannot stop the gut from producing more.
