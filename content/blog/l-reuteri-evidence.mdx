## The strain name is everything

There are hundreds of probiotic products marketed for infant colic. One probiotic strain has peer-reviewed, randomised controlled trial evidence for reducing infant crying duration. One.

**Lactobacillus reuteri DSM 17938**, sold commercially as BioGaia Protectis.

Not a generic Lactobacillus. Not a broad-spectrum probiotic. This strain, identified by its DSM deposit number: 17938. Any other strain does not carry this evidence. Any product that does not list this exact designation is not the intervention that was tested.

This distinction matters because parents spend weeks trying probiotic after probiotic with no result, concluding that probiotics do not work for colic. The correct conclusion is that a different strain does not work. The evidence for DSM 17938 is specific, replicable, and now confirmed across three independent research groups.

## The Savino et al. 2010 RCT

In 2010, Francesco Savino and colleagues published a randomised controlled trial in Pediatrics, the journal of the American Academy of Pediatrics. This is the foundational study.

**Study design.** Double-blind, placebo-controlled randomised controlled trial. The highest evidence standard in clinical research. Neither the parents nor the researchers knew which infants received L. reuteri DSM 17938 and which received placebo during the study period.

**Population.** 46 breastfed infants meeting the Wessel criteria for infantile colic. All infants were under 12 weeks of age.

**Intervention.** The L. reuteri group received 100 million colony forming units of L. reuteri DSM 17938 daily, administered as 5 drops. The placebo group received an identical preparation without the active strain.

**Primary outcome.** Daily crying duration measured in minutes, recorded by parents in structured diaries.

**Results at Day 21.** The L. reuteri group showed a median daily crying time of 35 minutes. The placebo group showed 90 minutes. This represents approximately a 74% reduction in crying duration in the probiotic group versus 38% in the placebo group.

**Secondary findings.** The L. reuteri group also showed reduced regurgitation frequency and improved stool frequency compared to placebo.

The study limitation acknowledged by the authors: the population was restricted to breastfed infants. The mechanism - gut microbiome modulation - was theorised as the operative pathway but not directly measured in this trial.

## Why two 2020 reviews matter more than the single study

A single RCT, regardless of quality, can be an outlier. Science advances by replication. The Savino 2010 finding has been replicated and independently reviewed by two separate research groups in 2020.

**Ellwood et al., BMJ Open, 2020.** A systematic review of systematic reviews - the highest level of evidence synthesis. The authors reviewed all available systematic reviews on interventions for infant colic and concluded that L. reuteri DSM 17938 carries the strongest evidence base of any reviewed intervention. This conclusion came from a research group independent of the original Savino team.

**Hjern et al., Acta Paediatrica, 2020.** A second independent systematic review published in the same year, reaching the same conclusion: moderate to strong evidence across multiple independent RCTs for reduced crying duration with L. reuteri DSM 17938.

Two independent research groups, two independent journals, the same year, the same conclusion. This is not one study from 2010. It is a converging evidence base across 16 years and three research groups.

## The mechanism

The theoretical mechanism operates through gut microbiome modulation. Colicky infants show elevated concentrations of gas-producing Proteobacteria and reduced concentrations of protective Lactobacillus species in their gut microbiome compared to non-colicky infants.

This imbalance creates a fermentation environment that produces continuous gas and inflammatory mediators. The inflammatory environment in the gut increases pain sensitivity - meaning the same amount of gas that would cause mild discomfort in a balanced microbiome causes acute pain in an inflamed one.

L. reuteri DSM 17938 colonises the gut, reduces Proteobacteria overgrowth, and decreases inflammatory marker production. This is the root cause correction, not symptom management. Gas drops (simethicone) break gas bubbles mechanically. They cannot change the environment producing the gas. L. reuteri DSM 17938 changes the environment.

The clinical timeline reflects this mechanism: results begin appearing at Day 7 to Day 10 and reach significance by Day 21. Parents expecting overnight results will stop the protocol before it has taken effect.

## What this does not tell you

**Formula-fed infants.** The Savino 2010 RCT enrolled breastfed infants specifically. The 2020 systematic reviews cover a broader evidence base including formula-fed populations, and the conclusions generally extend, but the primary evidence base was developed in breastfed infants. **Timing relative to feeds.** The 2010 study used daily administration but did not specify timing relative to feeding. Subsequent research and clinical experience suggest that timing relative to a feed affects colonisation efficiency. Getting the timing window wrong can reduce efficacy by a meaningful margin. **The dairy sensitivity differential.** Approximately 20% of breastfed colic cases trace primarily to cow's milk protein sensitivity rather than gut microbiome dysbiosis. These cases present with specific markers - green frothy stools, crying that worsens 4 to 24 hours after maternal dairy consumption, skin eczema. In these cases, maternal dairy elimination for 14 days is the first protocol to run, and L. reuteri DSM 17938 is secondary. Running L. reuteri without addressing a dairy sensitivity extends suffering.
